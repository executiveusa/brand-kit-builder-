## 10. VISUAL IDENTITY AND IMAGE STUDIO

### Asset-lock law

When a product, logo, person, package, artwork, or approved asset is supplied, start every generation specification with a lock statement equivalent to:

`Use the supplied asset unchanged. Preserve its proportions, details, colors, branding, materials, typography, and shape.`

Any requested change must be explicit and isolated.

### Image prompt architecture

Every production image prompt must contain:

1. Asset lock
2. One-sentence core concept
3. Intended use and aspect ratio
4. Composition and subject scale
5. Foreground, middleground, and background
6. Materials and texture
7. Lighting
8. Lens/camera or illustration treatment when relevant
9. Limited color palette tied to brand tokens
10. Environmental and cultural context
11. Authenticity requirements
12. Negative constraints
13. Accessibility and text-safe areas
14. Finishing standard
15. Version and seed/provenance notes when supported

Never use adjective piles in place of direction.

### Required visual systems

Create and document:

- master logo and responsive variants;
- clear space, minimum size, monochrome, reversed, favicon, app icon, social avatar, horizontal, vertical, symbol-only, and wordmark-only behavior;
- forbidden logo uses;
- typography system with licensed/open alternatives;
- semantic color system with accessible combinations;
- image art direction;
- illustration system;
- icon system;
- pattern and texture system;
- grid, spacing, radius, border, shadow, and elevation rules;
- motion identity;
- layout and composition principles;
- social, presentation, proposal, report, merchandise, packaging, and environmental applications relevant to the business;
- digital component and interface tokens.

### Originality and rights

- Never imitate a competitor's logo or protected identity.
- Reference brands only for principles, not replication.
- Verify font, icon, image, and template licenses.
- Record attribution requirements.
- Flag trademark clearance and legal review as human/legal work.
- Do not remove watermarks or ownership marks from third-party assets without verified rights.
- Store provenance for generated and sourced assets.

## 11. ICON LAW

Never use emojis as icons, controls, navigation, badges, feature symbols, status symbols, or decorative interface elements.

Priority:

1. Preserve the project's existing coherent icon system.
2. For new production web/app work, choose one family only:
   - Phosphor Icons for expressive consumer-facing work;
   - Radix Icons for compact utility/product interfaces.
3. For a compatible artifact host that already provides Tabler Icons, Tabler may be used.
4. When no library fits, create simple original SVG icons on a consistent grid.

Rules:

- verify license and pin the version;
- do not mix icon families on one surface;
- standardize optical size and stroke weight;
- use 16, 20, or 24 px systems unless the brand requires another documented scale;
- icon-only controls require accessible names;
- do not place every icon inside a colored rounded square;
- icons support labels; they do not replace clarity;
- polish must come from proportion, alignment, spacing, and interaction, not glow or decoration.

## 12. KAKU BRAND-BOOK STRUCTURE

The core brand book must preserve the KAKU sequence. Keep the sequence stable; adapt application media to the actual business.

### Core sequence

1. **Cover / identity**
   - Master identity and studio/client credit.

2. **Creative rationale**
   - The human, cultural, strategic, and emotional idea behind the system.

3. **Master logo reveal**
   - Present the primary mark cleanly and confidently.

4. **Symbol anatomy and meaning**
   - Explain each meaningful element and how the parts form one system.

5. **Logo system and uses**
   - Responsive variants, lockups, symbol, wordmark, orientation, clear space, minimum size, and use rules.

6. **Logo in context**
   - Show the identity in a meaningful environmental, service, campaign, or hero application.

7. **Typography**
   - Display and text families, hierarchy, weights, numbers, multilingual behavior, substitutes, and licensing.

8. **Color behavior**
   - Marks on key brand fields, monochrome, reversed, contrast, and background rules.

9. **Color palette and rationale**
   - Named colors, values, semantic roles, material or cultural origins, accessible pairings, and do/don't examples.

10. **Stationery / primary business collateral**
   - Business card, letterhead, proposal cover, invoice, email signature, or the closest real equivalent.

11. **Product, packaging, merchandise, or service application**
   - Use the most relevant real-world application. Do not invent packaging for a business that does not need it.

12. **Website / digital application**
   - Homepage or primary product surface showing navigation, hierarchy, CTA, typography, color, imagery, icons, and responsive intent.

13. **Closing / credits**
   - Ownership, version, date, asset locations, and contact/maintenance information.

### Required digital annexes

A. Brand strategy  
B. Brand voice  
C. Photography and image prompting  
D. Illustration, iconography, patterns, and textures  
E. Motion identity  
F. Social, presentation, proposal, and campaign templates  
G. Accessibility and localization  
H. Design tokens and component rules  
I. Governance, approvals, and change log  
J. Developer/design handoff

Outputs:

```text
brandbook.html
brandbook.pdf
brandbook-print.css
design-tokens.json
design-tokens.css
tailwind.tokens.js or equivalent when applicable
asset-manifest.json
```

The HTML brand book must open locally. The PDF must be rendered and visually verified page by page.
